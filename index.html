What is a standard?

What is the difference between Open Standards, Open Source, and Open Governance?

Open Standards / Open Source / Open Governance

Open Standards
  • Spec documents
  • Governance model
  • C#, ECMAScript, TCP
Open Source
  • Software
  • Licenses
  • .NET Runtime, ChakraCore, libsocket
Open Governance
  • Strategy, roadmap
  • Ecosystem
  • .NET Foundation, JS Foundation, Linux Foundation
Standards organizations aren't generally in the business of validating features. They merely ensure we all agree on the HOW.

“Standards are the distilled wisdom of people with expertise in their subject matter and who know the needs of the organizations they represent – people such as manufacturers, sellers, buyers, customers, trade associations, users or regulators.”

-- International Organization for Standardization (ISO)

Standards & Regulation

Standards are often codified in law (e.g. building codes).

Most jurisdictions require regulations based on open national or international standards where possible (e.g. the World Trade Organization).

Some standards are international standards in this sense (e.g. ISO standards). Others are less rigorous (e.g. IETF standards).

Is .NET a standard?

Pedantically, no.

  • The C# language is a standard (ECMA-334)
  • .NET Core is open source software
  • .NET Foundation provides some open governance, but .NET roadmap is driven by Microsoft

Elements of a Standard

  • Specification document and other artifacts
  • Venue
  • Governance model
    • Consensus process
    • IPR process & agreements

Standards Documents

An artifact that provides normative requirements for implementations to follow.

Commonly authored in HTML, plaintext, and Word documents.

Example standards documents
  • ECMA-334, the C# Language Specification
  • ECMA-262, the JavaScript Language Specficiation
  • ISO-8601, date and time format
  • RFC 2549, IP over Avian Carriers with QoS

Standards Venues

Venues provide structure including:

  • Standards organization (SDO)
  • Consensus process
  • Legal framework e.g. IPR agreements
  • Physical or virtual location to collaborate

Different venues have different approaches.

Consensus Process

How standards bodies make decisions as a group.

  • Pure Consensus: everyone agrees, no objections.
  • Rough Consensus: most delegates agree, objections are minimal.
  • Voting: Usually majority rules

Whether voting or not, getting consensus is a critical goal of standards participation!

Intellectual Policy Rights (IPR) policy

Patents: who owns the technology in a standard?

  • No patent policies (IETF)
  • RAND - reasonable and non-discriminatory (ISO)
  • RF - royalty free (ECMA TC39)

Venue: ECMA TC39

Owns the JavaScript Language

  • Consensus process: pure consensus
  • Legal Framework: RF patent grant
  • Location: In person, every 2 months

Venue: W3C HTML WG

Owns the HTML format

  • Consensus process: rough consensus
  • Legal Framework: RF patent grant
  • Location: Ad-hoc face-to-face, weekly calls

Venue: IETF

Owns RFCs

  • Consensus process: rough consensus
  • Legal Framework: Patent Disclosure
  • Location: Face-to-face 3x/year, online, mailing lists

Venue: ISO

International standards of all sorts

  • Consensus process: voting
  • Legal Framework: Patent Disclosure & RAND
  • Location: in person/virtual, general assembly (GA) once per year in person

Participating in Standards

New standards or existing standards have different approaches.

Making a New Standard

  1. Find a venue
  2. Define IPR policies
  3. Identify stakeholders
  4. Define a consensus process
  5. Write and publish artifacts

1. Find a Venue

Consider what you need:

  • Type of work?
  • IPR policy?
  • WTO rules?
  • Administrative support?

2. Define IPR policies

Often these will come with your choice of venue. Talk with CELA.

3. Identify Stakeholders

A standard is only useful when it reflects its stakeholders (e.g. users and other implementers). Find them and engage them in the process.

4. Define a consensus process

When, where, and how do you make decisions? How do you handle disagreements?

5. Write and publish artifacts

The end goal: a document everyone agrees with and follows.

Case Study: C#

Goal was (IMO) to find a standards body that works on programming languages, has established IPR policies, follows WTO guidelines, and a fair amount of process flexibility.

An ECMA TC fit the bill nicely.

Case Study: Promises

Goal is to drive interoperability in the JS library ecosystem, no IP concerns, mostly a documentation effort.

A github page fits this nicely.

It's fine to start small and set yourself up for an "official" standard. E.g. the Promises/A+ specification was eventually incorporated into ECMAScript.

Joining an Existing Standard

  1. Find its venue
  2. Reach out to participants inside Microsoft
  3. Read and agree to IPR and other policies
  4. Learn the consensus process
  5. Write and publish artifacts

Patterns for Successful Standards Participation

Standards work is hard work.

Implementors, vendors, and users will never agree on everything

It's as much about "people problems" as "technology problems".

Based on Patterns for Participation by Allen Wirfs-Brock

Pattern 1: Start by Learning

Standards are often extremely complex. Jumping in immediately can cause problems. Start by:

  1. Listening. Get an understanding of what is under discussion and group dynamics. Asking questions is always good.
  2. Research. Standards bodies are good at publishing decision history. Read it all.
  3. Network. Standards are political by nature. Introduce yourself to your peers, learn what people work on and their goals.

Pattern 2: Be explicit about your goals

Everyone comes to standards with different goals in mind, whether personal or professional. Be transparent about what you want to achieve and why.

Pattern 3: Contribute outside of Spec Authoring

Lots of work goes into standards, and believe it or not, most of it isn't writing the actual specification.

  1. Note-taking: maybe the best way to learn!
  2. Writing test cases: many standards have an official test suite as well.
  3. Writing documentation: there are always opportunities to make what you learned easier for the next person.
  4. Help with administrative tasks like meeting planning or facilitation.

Pattern 4: Growth-Mindset-driven Collaboration

Standards work best when participants know how to pick the right battles, admit fault, and compromise.

Remember: consensus doesn't imply agreement.

Pattern 5: Identify allies early

Advancing a standard is easier with friends. Find people who share your goals and author joint proposals.

Pattern 6: Have a back-pocket alternative

Sometimes the committee is headed in a non-desirable direction. Arguing for a change of course is often difficult.

The back-pocket alternative illustrates a concrete alternative. As problems with the consensus approach arise, the back-pocket alternative is ready to jump in.

Pattern 7: Maximally minimal Proposals

Consensus is hard. Consensus in giant features is harder. Pare away all but essential details.

Create clear carve-outs for future work on areas of disagreement.

Pattern 8: Break consensus as a last resort

Don't "throw yourself on the tracks" or "die on the mountain" unless you have an extremely good reason.

Breaking consensus comes with political cost. Years of work from other delegates may be at risk.
